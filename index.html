From tangled kelp forests to vibrant coral reefs, the vast majority of marine life lives in brightly lit water shallower than 200 m (656 ft).

As depth increases, fewer colors remain visible. Red animals look dull and blend easily into their gloomy suroundings.

Beyond 1000 m (3281 ft), there is no sunlight at all. Bioluminescent organisms are the only source of light in the cold, neverending darkness.

The Cuvierâ€™s beaked whale, the deepest diving mammal, can travel 3000 m (9843 ft) below the surface in search of deep-sea squid.

A sunken whale carcass provides a great amount of nutrients for life at the bottom of the ocean. A whale fall can support a community for years.

At 7966 m (26135 ft) down, the tiny snailfish is the deepest fish ever found. Their proteins have adapted to resist collapse under intense pressure.

Hydrothermal vents are volcanic fissures on the sea floor which emit heated, mineral-rich water. They are the foundation of dense ecosystems.
